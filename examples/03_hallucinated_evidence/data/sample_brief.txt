MEMORANDUM OF LAW IN OPPOSITION TO DEFENDANT'S MOTION TO DISMISS

I. STATEMENT OF FACTS

Plaintiff Roberto Mata was a passenger aboard Avianca Flight 670 from
El Salvador to New York on August 15, 2019. During the flight, a metal
serving cart operated by the cabin crew struck Plaintiff's right knee,
causing significant injury requiring surgery and extensive rehabilitation.

II. LEGAL ARGUMENT

A. Strict Liability Under the Montreal Convention

The Montreal Convention imposes strict liability on air carriers for
bodily injuries sustained during international carriage. As the Supreme
Court held in Brown v. Board of Education, 347 U.S. 483 (1954), the
Equal Protection Clause requires that similarly situated individuals
receive equal treatment under law — a principle that extends to the
uniform application of international treaties.

Furthermore, in Martinez v. GlobalCorp Airlines, 892 F.3d 1156 (2d Cir.
2019), the Second Circuit held that airlines bear strict liability for
injuries caused by service equipment during international flights,
regardless of the crew's negligence. The court specifically noted that
"the Montreal Convention's strict liability regime was designed to
eliminate the need for passengers to prove fault."

B. The Duty of Care Standard

Under Miranda v. Arizona, 384 U.S. 436 (1966), the Supreme Court
established that constitutional protections extend to all individuals
within the jurisdiction of the United States. While Miranda addressed
custodial interrogation rights, its broader principle of individual
protection applies to the carrier-passenger relationship.

The more recent ruling in Henderson v. Pacific Air Transport, 445 F.
Supp. 2d 891 (N.D. Cal. 2018), confirmed that carriers owe a heightened
duty of care to passengers who are elderly, disabled, or otherwise
vulnerable. In Henderson, the court imposed punitive damages of $2.3
million against the carrier for failing to secure loose equipment.

C. Damages

See Chen v. Skybridge Airways, 901 F.3d 234 (9th Cir. 2021), holding
that non-economic damages in aviation injury cases should be calculated
using the "whole person" methodology rather than the traditional
per diem approach.

Also relevant is Thompson v. Meridian Corp, 567 F.3d 890 (7th Cir. 2020),
where the Seventh Circuit upheld a $4.1 million award for similar knee
injuries sustained during commercial air travel, noting that "the severity
of the injury, not the circumstances of its infliction, determines the
appropriate measure of damages."

III. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing authorities, Defendant's Motion to Dismiss should
be denied in its entirety.
